01

WGS84 — World Geodetic System 1984

WGS84 (World Geodetic System 1984) is the standard coordinate reference system used globally by GPS, Google Maps, and virtually all modern geospatial technology. It defines the Earth as an ellipsoid — not a perfect sphere — with precisely measured dimensions.

Key WGS84 Parameters:

Semi-Major Axis (a)
6,378,137.0 m
Equatorial radius
Semi-Minor Axis (b)
6,356,752.3142 m
Polar radius
Flattening (1/f)
298.257223563
Inverse flattening
EPSG Code
EPSG:4326
Geographic 2D CRS

WGS84 coordinates are expressed as Latitude (north–south, −90° to +90°) and Longitude (east–west, −180° to +180°). Kenya lies between approximately 4°N–5°S latitude and 34°E–42°E longitude.

WGS84 Reference Ellipsoid — Earth approximated as oblate spheroid
02

Geographic vs Projected Coordinate Systems

There are two fundamentally different types of coordinate systems used in surveying and GIS work.

⊕ Geographic CRS

Coordinates expressed in degrees (latitude/longitude). Represent angular positions on the 3D ellipsoid. Units are degrees, minutes, seconds.

●
Use for: GPS output, global data exchange, data storage, web mapping APIs (Google Maps, Leaflet default).
Example (Nairobi CBD)
-1.286389°, 36.817223°
◈ Projected CRS

Coordinates expressed in metres on a flat 2D plane. Uses a mathematical projection to "flatten" the ellipsoid. Units are always metres.

●
Use for: area/distance calculations, CAD work, engineering surveys, construction setout.
Example (Nairobi CBD, UTM 37S)
E 257,098 / N 9,857,766
▲
Critical: Never mix coordinate systems. Always verify which CRS a dataset uses before importing into another. A common error in Kenya is mixing Arc 1960 local datum coordinates with WGS84, which can introduce errors of 200–300 metres.
03

Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) Projection

UTM divides the world into 60 vertical zones, each 6° of longitude wide. Within each zone, the Transverse Mercator projection creates a flat map with minimal distortion. Scale factor at the central meridian is 0.9996.

Each UTM zone has a False Easting of 500,000 m (to keep all values positive) and a False Northing of 10,000,000 m for zones in the Southern Hemisphere.

Kenya spans UTM Zones 36, 37 and 38. Zone 37 covers the majority of the country including Nairobi.
04

Kenya UTM Zones in Detail

Kenya is divided across three UTM zones. The equator divides each zone into North (N) and South (S) hemispheres. Most of Kenya's population centres fall in the Southern hemisphere zones.

Zone 36 — Western Kenya
Longitude: 30°E – 36°E
Central Meridian: 33°E
Hemisphere: 36S / 36N
EPSG (S): 32636
EPSG (N): 32236
Covers: Kakamega, Kisumu, Siaya, Busia, Trans Nzoia, Uasin Gishu (Eldoret), Kericho, Kisii, Nyamira, Vihiga, Bungoma, Homa Bay, Migori
Zone 37 — Central Kenya
Longitude: 36°E – 42°E
Central Meridian: 39°E
Hemisphere: 37S / 37N
EPSG (S): 32637
EPSG (N): 32237
Covers: Nairobi, Kiambu, Nakuru, Nyandarua, Nyeri, Kirinyaga, Muranga, Thika, Embu, Machakos, Makueni, Kajiado, Narok, Laikipia, Samburu, Marsabit, Isiolo, Meru, Tharaka-Nithi
Zone 38 — Eastern/Coastal Kenya
Longitude: 42°E – 48°E
Central Meridian: 45°E
Hemisphere: 38S / 38N
EPSG (S): 32638
EPSG (N): 32238
Covers: Mombasa, Kwale, Kilifi, Lamu, Tana River, Garissa, Wajir, Mandera. Also portions of Malindi and the coast belt.
▲
Zone boundary caution: Projects that straddle UTM zone boundaries (e.g. near 36°E or 42°E) should use a custom projection or reproject all data to a single zone before analysis.

EPSG Code Reference for Kenya

EPSG (European Petroleum Survey Group) codes are standardised identifiers for coordinate reference systems. Used in QGIS, ArcGIS, PostGIS, PROJ, and all major GIS platforms. The EPSG registry is maintained by the IOGP.

EPSG Code Name Type Datum Use in Kenya
EPSG:4326 WGS 84 Geographic WGS 1984 GPS output, web maps, data exchange
EPSG:32636 WGS 84 / UTM Zone 36S Projected WGS 1984 Western Kenya surveys (Kisumu, Eldoret)
EPSG:32637 WGS 84 / UTM Zone 37S Projected WGS 1984 Central Kenya (Nairobi, Nakuru, Nyeri)
EPSG:32638 WGS 84 / UTM Zone 38S Projected WGS 1984 Coast / Eastern Kenya (Mombasa, Lamu)
EPSG:32237 WGS 84 / UTM Zone 37N Projected WGS 1984 Northern Kenya north of equator (Marsabit, Turkana)
EPSG:21036 Arc 1960 / UTM Zone 36S Legacy Arc 1960 Legacy government maps (pre-GPS era)
EPSG:21037 Arc 1960 / UTM Zone 37S Legacy Arc 1960 Legacy cadastral records, Kenya Land Registry
EPSG:4210 Arc 1960 (Geographic) Legacy Arc 1960 Old topographic maps, Kenya 1:50,000 series
EPSG:3857 WGS 84 / Pseudo-Mercator Web WGS 1984 Google Maps, OpenStreetMap tiles, web display only
⚠
Arc 1960 vs WGS84: The Kenya Land Registry historically used the Arc 1960 datum. Coordinates in Arc 1960 can differ from WGS84 by approximately 200–380 metres depending on location. Always apply a datum transformation when mixing these systems.

GIS Best Practices

1
Always document the CRS. Every dataset must have its coordinate reference system clearly recorded in metadata. Without this, data becomes unreliable.
2
Store raw data in WGS84 (EPSG:4326). Use geographic coordinates for archiving and data exchange. Reproject to UTM only when doing calculations that require metric units.
3
Never calculate area or distance in geographic coordinates. Area calculated from decimal degrees gives incorrect results. Always reproject to UTM first, then calculate.
4
Handle datum transformation carefully. When combining Arc 1960 legacy data with modern WGS84 GPS data, always apply a proper datum shift. In QGIS, use the PROJ Helmert transformation parameters for Kenya.
5
Use on-the-fly projection (OTF) carefully. QGIS can reproject layers on-the-fly for display, but data quality depends on each layer having correct CRS assigned — not assumed.
6
Validate GPS data before processing. Check for outliers (points in the ocean, wildly incorrect elevations, duplicate coordinates) before integrating GPS field data into a project.
7
Match your UTM zone to your project area. Don't use Zone 37S for a Kisumu project — use Zone 36S. Using the wrong zone introduces increasing distortion with distance from the central meridian.
08

Kenya Survey Recommendations

Specific recommendations for survey work in Kenya based on Survey of Kenya standards and EPSG best practices.

⊕ GPS / GNSS Field Work
• Collect in WGS84 (default GPS output)
• Minimum 5 satellites for survey-grade
• PDOP < 4 for topographic surveys
• RTK: tie to nearest CORS station
• Static: minimum 2-hour occupation
• Always record ellipsoidal heights
◈ Drone / UAV Surveys
• GCPs in WGS84 for PPK/RTK processing
• Minimum 5 GCPs per block
• Verify check points after processing
• Export orthophoto in UTM (EPSG:32637)
• Report accuracy in RMS error (cm)
• Comply with KCAA regulations
⊞ Cadastral / Title Survey
• Use UTM with correct zone
• Submit to Kenya Land Registry in Arc 1960
• Apply Helmert parameters for datum shift
• Tie to nearest primary trig station
• Third Order accuracy: ±5cm linear
• Use Survey of Kenya approved benchmarks
✈ Engineering Surveys
• Local grid tied to national control
• Scale factor for UTM zones noted
• Use KeRIS data where available
• Road/infrastructure: UTM Zone 37S
• Datum: WGS84 (modern projects)
• Always provide transformation report
✓
Regulatory note: All geospatial work in Kenya should comply with the Survey Act (Cap. 299), Land Survey Regulations, and instructions from the Director of Surveys, Survey of Kenya. Large projects require a licensed surveyor.
